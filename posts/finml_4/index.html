Sample Weights

Chapter 4: Sample Weights

Sample Weights

  • Most financial time series literature and models assume IID processes
  • Financial time series are NOT IID
  • Overlapping outcomes for our labeling process will mean that our observations are also not IID
  • We have overlapping outcomes when one period’s label and returns coincide with another intervals
  • For a given time point t we may have several labels running concurrently.
  • We need a some function to compute how many labels we have running per time step
  • Uniqueness of label for each feature

Bagging Classifiers and Uniqueness

  • Assuming IID observations leads to oversampling
  • In-bag observations will become redundant to each other and similar to out of bag observations
  • Redundancy of draws makes the bootstram inefficient
  • For RF, all trees in the forest will be similar copies of overfit decision tree
  • Out of bag samples will be similar to in-bag samples but so out of bag accuracy will be overestimated
  • You can put an upper limit on the number of random samples so as to reduce the chance of oversampling

Sequential Bootstrap

  • Resample sequentially until K distinct original samples appear
  • What are we sampling here?
  • Let’s call it some kind of gestalt. There is a pattern that leads to a return.
  • We know the pattern charactertistics and the return
  • So our y’s are our returns
  • Our observations are our patterns
  • Maybe each of the observations has as features different bar characteristics, volumue bars, tick bars, time bars
  • We have a bunch of these little snippets
  • We want to weight observations by absolute return that can be attributed to it
  • When labels are a function of the return’s sign {-1, 1} or meta-labeling {0,1} sample weights can be defined in terms of attributed returns over the events lifespan

Time Decay

  • As adaptive systems, markets evolve
  • older examples will be less relevant than new ones
  • Sample weights for older observations should be lower than newer observations
  • We need a decay function that tells us how to reduce the weights on certain observations
  • We can have weights never decay, weights that decay but stay positive and weights that decay to zero

Class weights

  • For rare or underrepresented classes of events we want to increase our samples from this type of event
